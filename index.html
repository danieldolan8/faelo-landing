A personal story

A story of luck.

I'm Italian. I live in Brussels. Last year I was in Berlin — a close friend happened to be flying in the same day. We only found out because of a Strava notification. We spent the whole day together.

A month later, a friend ran the London Marathon while I was there. She didn't know I was. We found out that evening on Instagram.

Same story. Different feeling. One was a great day. One was a missed one.

The only difference was luck. Caila removes the luck.

Why it matters

Social isolation among young people is not an anecdote. It is a documented and accelerating trend.

In Europe, the share of young people who don't socialise even once a week has risen from one in ten to one in four.
People in their teens and twenties now hang out about as much as someone ten years older than them did in the past.
Time-use data from the US and UK shows a marked increase in time spent alone among teens and young adults over the past decade, with little to no change among older groups.
More time spent alone is strongly associated with lower life satisfaction — and this deterioration in young people's life satisfaction between 2010 and 2023 can be substantially explained by changes in how they spend their time.
The most meaningful activities — socialising, shared experiences — are being replaced by solitary screen time, which young people themselves rate as the least meaningful of all activities.

Every major social platform has accelerated this trend. They are optimised for engagement, not connection. For followers, not friends. For the past, not the future.

Most social networks today are retrospective. You look back at things that happened and feel disconnected, guilty, like you missed out. Caila is the opposite — it's forward-looking, connecting people to moments before they pass rather than after.
